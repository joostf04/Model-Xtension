\section{Context and Motivation}
% ============================================================

Variable selection in high dimensional regressions is a frequently occurring problem in econometrics and beyond. Two recent approaches attack this from different angles, with one applying the \citet{candes2018} Model-X Knockoffs framwework.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=4pt]
    \item \citet{fan2020ipad} applies Model-X Knockoffs framework with a factor model for the covariate
distribution to achieve FDR-controlled variable selection.
    \item \citet{chudik2024ocmt} studies the \textit{One Covariate at a
Time Multiple Testing} procedure under time-varying parameters. Shows OCMT selects
variables with non-zero average effects and recommends full-sample selection with
down-weighted forecasting. Provides no FDR control.
\end{enumerate}

Mahrad Sharifvaghefi is a co-author on both papers. These represent two lines of work by
the same researcher attacking the same problem. No paper compares them or asks: under
parameter instability, when should a forecaster prefer FDR-controlled selection (IPAD)
over threshold-based selection (OCMT), and can we get the best of both, which is what this proposal/thesis aims to adress.

I have divided this into 3 smaller research questions.

\newpage
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{RQ\arabic*.}, itemsep=6pt]
    \item \textbf{Diagnostic.} How does parameter instability affect the FDR control and power of IPAD knockoffs? How does it affect the false discovery behaviour of OCMT (as OCMT has no formal FDR guarantee)?

    \item \textbf{Comparative.} Under structural breaks, which method provides better variable selection for forecasting; IPAD (FDR control and assumes stability) or OCMT (manages instability and no FDR control)?

    \item \textbf{Constructive.}Can discounted IPAD retain meaningful power toward the post-break
 │ regime, and how does the resulting power–FDR trade-off compare to rolling-window IPAD and the oracle?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methodology}
% ============================================================

Before we dive into the models, it is important to show that the models can not be compared in terms of FDR under the same null hypothesis.

\subsection{Dual Null}

Say variable $j$ has $\beta_j = 0.5$ pre-break and $\beta_j = 0$ post-break. If you're forecasting tomorrow after the break, that variable is noise: selecting it is a false discovery fron a forecasting standpoint. But if you ask ``did this variable ever matter?'', the answer is yes: its time-averaged coefficient is $0.25$. OCMT and standard IPAD would select it (although for different reasons) and call that a success. The additional models I propose would call it a false discovery. This is formalised in table below.

To revisit the example before the table, this variable $j$ would be a true discovery under $H_0^{avg}$ and  $H_0^{\text{full}}$ , which is OCMT's and standard IPAD perspective respectively, but a false discovery under $H_0^{(2)}$, which is the discounted IPAD or forecasting perspective. This also shows why the models behave differently under breaks.

\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, label=\textbf{A\arabic*.}]
    \item \textbf{Stable $P_X$.} The covariate distribution does not change at the break. Only $\beta_t$ changes. This isolates the coefficient instability channel and ensures knockoff validity.
    \item \textbf{Single break.} The simplest form of instability.
    \item \textbf{Cross-sectional independence across $t$.} Observations are i.i.d.\ draws from the factor model within each period. 
    \item \textbf{Known target FDR level.} $q = 0.20$ throughout (standard in the knockoff literature).
\end{enumerate}

As mentioned above \textbf{A1} is necessary for knockoff validity, however, some violations are inevitable in empirical experiments. \citet{barber2020} show that FDR is still bounded under misspecified $P_X$, although by a higher bound ($q \to q \cdot e^\epsilon$). 

\subsection{Theoretical Foundation (\citet{candes2018}'s Model-X Knockoffs)}

Given i.i.d.\ observations $(X_i, Y_i)$, construct knockoff variables $\tilde{X}$ satisfying:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt]
    \item Pairwise exchangeability: $(X, \tilde{X})_{\text{swap}(S)} \stackrel{d}{=} (X, \tilde{X})$ for all $S \subseteq \{1,\ldots,p\}$
    \item Conditional independence: $\tilde{X} \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid X$
\end{itemize}

Feature importance statistics $W_j$ satisfy the sign-flip property, and the knockoff+ filter at target level $q$ provides finite-sample FDR control: $\text{FDR} \leq q$.

The knockoff construction depends only on $P_X$, not on $P_{Y|X}$. Thus, if stable $P_X$  but $\beta_t$ changes, knockoffs remain valid, only the signal detection step is affected.

\subsection{Baselines}
\subsubsection{Standard IPAD \citep{fan2020ipad} \textit{(my "direct" replication, will use same DGP)}}
 
IPAD  applies knockoffs for economic data by modelling the covariate distribution with a latent factor model:
\begin{equation}
    X_t = BF_t + U_t, \qquad F_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_r), \quad U_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_U),
\end{equation}
where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times r}$ is the loading matrix and $\Sigma_U$ is diagonal. Number of factors are estimated using \citet{ahn2013} ratio test, which gives an estimate $\hat{\Sigma}_X = \hat{B}\hat{B}' + \hat{\Sigma}_U$ from which knockoffs are constructed.

The feature importance statistic is the LCD (Lasso Coefficient Difference):
\begin{equation}
    W_j = |\hat{\beta}_j| - |\hat{\beta}_{j+p}|,
\end{equation}
where $(\hat{\beta}_1, \ldots, \hat{\beta}_{2p})$ is the Lasso estimate on the $[X, \tilde{X}]$ with cross-validated $\lambda$.  The knockoff$+$ filter is applied at $q = 0.20$. Standard IPAD assumes stable $\beta$ and targets $H_0^{\text{full}}$.

The empirical application uses 100 knockff repetitions for forecast averaging and selection frequency. Although \citet{nguyen2020} show that aggregating knockoff realisations improves power, I leave it as a natural extension.

\subsubsection{OCMT, \citet{chudik2018, chudik2024ocmt}}

Tests each covariate individually: regress $Y$ on $X_j$, obtain $t$-statistic, threshold at
$$c_p(N, \delta) = \Phi^{-1}\!\left(1 - \frac{\alpha}{2\,N^\delta}\right),$$
where $\alpha$ is the nominal significance level (e.g.\ $\alpha = 0.05$), $N$ is the number of candidate covariates, and $\delta > 0$ is a penalisation exponent. \citet{chudik2024ocmt} recommend $\delta = 1$, "yielding a Bonferonni like correction ($\alpha/N$ per test, two-sided)".

Under coefficient stability, OCMT consistently selects all signals and excludes all noise variables as $T \to \infty$. Under a structural break, \citet{chudik2024ocmt} show that OCMT selects variables whose time-averagedeffect is non-zero, i.e. targeting the $H_0^{\text{avg}}$ defined in Table \ref{tab:dualnull} . 
They recommend to apply down-weighting at the \emph{forecasting} stage only (exponentially weighted OLS on the selected variables), leaving the selection step unweighted. No formal FDR control is provided or claimed.

\subsection{Proposed Methods}

\subsubsection{Discounted IPAD (Proposed Modification)}

Idea is to replace the standard Lasso with an exponentially weighted Lasso:
\[
    \hat{\beta}^{(\lambda,\delta)} = \underset{b}{\arg\min}\ \sum_{t=1}^T \delta^{T-t}\bigl(Y_t - [X_t, \tilde{X}_t]'\, b\bigr)^2 + \lambda\|b\|_1.
\]

To implement this, multiply observation $t$ by weight $\sqrt{\delta^{T-1-t}}$, then fit standard Lasso on the reweighted data.

\begin{proposition}[Sign-flip property of weighted LCD]
If $w_t > 0$ are deterministic weights constructed independently of $\tilde{X}$, then the weighted LCD statistic $W_j^{(w)} = |\hat{\beta}_j^{(w)}| - |\hat{\beta}_{j+p}^{(w)}|$ satisfies the sign-flip property.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Define reweighted data $(Y^w, Z^w)$ where $Y_t^w = w_t Y_t$ and $Z_t^w = w_t[X_t, \tilde{X}_t]$. Since $w_t$ is deterministic and independent of $\tilde{X}$:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), itemsep=2pt]
    \item \emph{Conditional independence:} $\tilde{X} \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid X$ implies $Z^w_{\cdot,j+p} \perp\!\!\!\perp Y^w \mid Z^w_{\cdot,1:p}$ (weighting preserves conditional independence).
    \item \emph{Column exchangeability:} $(X,\tilde{X})_{\text{swap}(S)} \stackrel{d}{=} (X,\tilde{X})$ implies $(Z^w_{\cdot,1:p}, Z^w_{\cdot,p+1:2p})_{\text{swap}(S)} \stackrel{d}{=} (Z^w_{\cdot,1:p}, Z^w_{\cdot,p+1:2p})$ since the same row-scaling applies to both original and knockoff columns.
    \item Therefore $W_j^{(w)}(Y^w,[Z^w]_{\text{swap}(S)}) = -W_j^{(w)}(Y^w,Z^w)$ for $j \in S$, by the standard sign-flip argument. \qedhere
\end{enumerate}
\end{proof}
\begin{remark}[FDR guarantee]
Since the weighted LCD statistics satisfy the sign-flip property, the knockoff$+$ filter applied to $\{W_j^{(w)}\}_{j=1}^p$ at target level $q$ inherits the finite-sample FDR guarantee of \citet{candes2018}, Theorem~3.4: $\text{FDR} \leq q$. The guarantee holds for any fixed, deterministic weight sequence $\{w_t\}$, including the exponential weights $w_t = \sqrt{\delta^{T-1-t}}$ used in discounted IPAD. 

 This is a \textbf{conditional} statement: FDR is controlled only if the knockoff construction is valid (i.e.\ $P_X$ is correctly specified), which Assumption~A1 ensures.
\end{remark}

 The weighted Lasso behaves as if operating on
$$n_{\text{eff}} = \frac{\left(\sum_{t=1}^{T} w_t^2\right)^2}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} w_t^4} =
 \frac{\left(\frac{1-\delta^T}{1-\delta}\right)^2}{\frac{1-\delta^{2T}}{1-\delta^2}} =
 \frac{(1-\delta^T)(1+\delta)}{(1-\delta)(1+\delta^T)}$$
effective observations.For large $T$ where $\delta^T \approx 0$, this simplifies to:

 $$n_{\text{eff}} \approx \frac{1+\delta}{1-\delta}$$

This means, for $T=500$, $n_{\text{eff}} = 39, (\delta =0,95)$, so $n_{\text{eff}} << 2p$, which makes selection unreliable and a 'zero-variable selection' possible, giving an FDR of 0/0, i.e. 0. To prevent reporting a distorted FDR image, any configuration averaging less than one discovery across $M$ replications will be flagged as zero power and excluded from FDR stats. $\delta = 0.99$ nearly reaches $n_{\text{eff}} > 2p$, giving reliable seperation. Thus any lower delta can be seen as a stress test rather than recommendation. 


\textbf{Discount factor selection.}  In the simulation study, results are reported for each fixed $\delta \in {0.95, 0.97, 0.98, 0.99, 1.00}$ separately,
 │ preserving the formal FDR guarantee. The cross-validated $\delta$ is used only in the empirical application, where
 │ the FDR guarantee is treated as approximate. 

 \subsubsection{Rolling-Window IPAD}
 
An alternative localisation strategy. Knockoffs are constructed from the full sample (since $P_X$ is assumed stable), but the LCD statistics are computed within a trailing window of size $h$. The knockoff$+$ filter is then applied to the windowed LCD values.

Restricting the Lasso to a trailing window $\{T-h+1, \ldots, T\}$ is equivalent to fitting on a row-subset of $[X, \tilde{X}]$. Since knockoff pairs $(X_j, \tilde{X}_j)$ are exchangeable across all rows jointly (by construction) and $\tilde{X} \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid X$ holds on any row-subset, row-selection preserves both properties (given it is done without looking at $\tilde{X}$). Thus, the windowed LCD statistics inherit the sign-flip property, and the knockoff$+$ filter controls FDR at level $q$.

Rolling-window IPAD has $n_{\text{eff}} = h$ (e.g.\ $150$ at $h = 0.3T$), vs. $n_{\text{eff}} \approx 66$ fo r discounted IPAD at $\delta = 0.97$. Rolling windows thus retain more power at the cost of less emphasis on more recent (possibly post vs. pre break observations).

Window sizes: $h \in \{0.3T,\; 0.5T,\; 0.7T\}$. This method localises through hard truncation rather than smooth weighting, providing a comparison point for discounted IPAD.

\subsection{Benchmarks}

\subsubsection{Down-Weighted OCMT}
 
The approach recommended by \citet{chudik2024ocmt}: full-sample (unweighted) OCMT for variable selection, followed by exponentially weighted OLS for post-selection forecasting. This separates selection and forecasting, down-weighting only at the forecasting stage. It is a benchmark for whether localising the selection step (as discounted IPAD does) offers an advantage over localising only the forecasting step.

\subsubsection{Oracle}
 
Knows the break date $t^*$. Uses the full sample for knockoff construction ($P_X$ stable) but restricts the LCD statistics to post-break observations only. This provides an upper bound on what any localised method can achieve without knowledge of the break timing.

\section{Simulation Design}
\subsection{DGP}
Following \citet{fan2020ipad} (which I thus intend to replicate), the covariate distribution is stable throughout and follows a latent factor model:
\begin{equation}
    X_t = BF_t + U_t, \qquad F_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_r), \quad U_t \sim \mathcal{N}\!\bigl(0,\, \text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_p^2)\bigr),
\end{equation}
with $r = 3$ factors, loadings $B_{jk} \sim U(-1,1)$, and idiosyncratic variances $\sigma_j^2 \sim U(0.5, 1.5)$. Each column of $X$ is rescaled to have $\textit{l}_2$ norm one, after which the response is generated as $Y_t = X_t'\beta_t + \varepsilon_t$, $\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, where $\beta_t$ is subject to a single structural break at $t^*$.

\subsection{Scenarios}
 
The primary scenario is signal entry/exit: the active sets $S^{(1)}$ and $S^{(2)}$ differ across time, with $|S^{(1)}| = |S^{(2)}| = s$ and overlap $|S^{(1)} \cap S^{(2)}|$ varying from full $s$ to none. Non-zero coefficients are set to $\beta_j = \sqrt{\frac{AT}{s}}$  With $\text{Var}(\varepsilon_t) = 1$ and near orthogonal signals, the population signal-to-noise ratio is:
 $$\text{SNR} = \text{Var}(X_t'\beta_t) \approx \sum_{j \in S} \frac{AT}{s} \cdot \frac{1}{T} = A$$

The grid uses $A \in \{2, 4\}$, corresponding to $\text{SNR} \in \{2, 4\}$. A stable baseline ($\beta$ constant) serves as a sanity check, verifying that all methods perform as they should in absence of breaks. Two secondary scenarios, magnitude shift ($\beta^{(2)} = c \cdot \beta^{(1)}$, same support) and sign reversal ($\beta^{(2)} = -\beta^{(1)}$), are included if time permits.

\subsection{Simulation Grid}
The configuration uses $(T, p, s) = (500, 200, 20)$. The grid varies: break location $\tau \in \{0.5, 0.7\}$, break at $\tau T$; overlap fraction $\in \{0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$; signal-to-noise ratio $\in \{2, 4\}$; and discount factor $\delta \in \{0.95, 0.97, 0.99, 1.00\}$. This yields $2 \times 5 \times 2 \times 4 = 80$ cells, each evaluated across six methods with $M = 500$ replications. I understand the computational demand and therefore have already started running simulations.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*), itemsep=4pt]
    \item Empirical FDR against $H_0^{(2)}$ (the forecasting-relevant null).
    \item Empirical FDR against $H_0^{\text{avg}}$.
    \item Power (true positive rate) against $H_0^{(2)}$.
    \item Out-of-sample $R^2_{\text{OOS}}$ from one-step-ahead forecasts using post-selection OLS.
\end{enumerate}
Evaluating each method against both nulls is essential: it ensures the comparison is not a consequence of mismatched evaluation criteria. 

\section{Empirical Application (\textit{Only if Time Permits})}

Monthly equity premium $r_{t+1}^e = r_{t+1}^{\text{mkt}} - r_f$; 14 standard \citet{welch2008} variables plus 3 lags of each ($p = 56$); sample 1950:01--2023:12; OOS evaluation 1966:01--2023:12 (expanding window, minimum 15 years in-sample). At each forecast origin $t$, apply: (i) IPAD, (ii) OCMT, (iii) Discounted IPAD. $\delta$ is selected from $\{0.95, 0.97, 0.98, 0.99, 1.00\}$ by minimising the mean squared forecast error over the preceding 60 months using post-selection weighted OLS. Since $\delta$ is chosen from the data, the formal FDR guarantee of Proposition 1 (which requires fixed, pre-specified weights) no longer holds exactly; FDR control should be interpreted as approximate in this setting.

Output will be focussed on \citet{campbell2008} $R^2_{OOS}$'s, and examining if the methods pick up parameter instability, i.e. does variable selection shift around breaks (e.g. 2008, COVID).

\subsection{Waarom OCMT als geen formele FDR control?} 

OCMT handles instabiliteit van parameters. IPAD wél formele FDR control, niet gemaakt voor instabiliteit van parameters. OCMT kan juist deze instabiliteit handlen, maar biedt geen formele fdr control.

\subsection{Kans dat OCMT toch FDR control geeft}

Bij stabiele $\beta$ en sparse matrix, i.e. weinig signalen t.o.v. $T$. In dit geval, genoeg power per variabele wegens stabiliteit en dus gaat FDR richting 0.

FDR (en dus control) hangt af van $H_0$  (avg of 2). Bij variabele die pre break non-zero is maar post break 0, zou het denk ik bij $H_0^{avg}$ impliciet kunnen, bij $H_0^2$ verwacht ik het niet.

\subsection{Wat is 'better' variable selection?}

In esssentie zie ik beter als een betere FDR-power trade-off met oog op forecasten, dus zou de focus op $R^2_{OOS}$ zijn, maar zijn wellicht de power en FDR statistieken informatiever.

Primsrily OOS $R^2$, uiteindelijk is de reden voor interesse forecasting. Daarnaast power vs $H_0^2$ (vind de methode de variabelen die nu actief zijn) en empirischew FDR vs $H_0^2$.

\subsection{Wat als meer dan n covariates geselecteerd in de eerste stap}
